### AI in Power & Utilities: The Explosion and the Drought (part 2) Every leap in artificial intelligence has come with a hidden cost. As models grow in capability, they grow in demand for infrastructure. Each generation of frontier models consumes more compute than

the one before it. Compute consumes energy. AI does not simply require smart software. It requires physical infrastructure --- hardware, power, cooling, and interconnects. These requirements are growing at a pace the grid has not yet absorbed. The training of frontier models like GPT-3 and GPT-4 required energy footprints that exceeded those of many industrial processes. These models were trained over months using tens of thousands of GPUs running at high utilization. Each GPU draws hundreds of watts, often in dense clusters, and runs in data centers that must maintain thermal equilibrium. The result is a load profile that is continuous, concentrated, and growing. The latest estimates show that power draw for training frontier AI models is doubling every twelve months. The pace of this growth is faster than the growth of most other digital systems. The internet took decades to reach its current scale of bandwidth and storage. AI is accelerating at a different rate. It is being driven not only by academic curiosity, but by market forces. Language models are becoming the backbone of search engines, productivity tools, and enterprise platforms. Image generation models are being embedded in marketing, design, and media workflows. The demand for training is not slowing. It is becoming institutionalized. For utilities, this trend carries direct implications. Every new model requires training. Every trained model requires inference. Every inference request triggers a computation. When those models are integrated into customer platforms or operational systems, the demand becomes persistent. It becomes part of the load that utilities must plan for, price, and support. This creates a new class of energy customer. Unlike traditional industrial users, who operate within clear schedules and physical constraints, AI-driven loads are elastic and irregular. They surge when new models are released or fine-tuned. They shift locations when compute becomes cheaper or more abundant elsewhere. They create clusters of demand in regions with cheap power or favorable regulation. These patterns are not predictable using traditional industrial growth models. At the same time, AI-driven energy use is hard to meter at a granular level. It can be embedded inside existing data centers. It can scale rapidly through cloud deployments. It can mask itself behind abstract services that do not disclose the nature of the underlying computation. This makes forecasting more difficult. It creates blind spots in regional planning. And it raises the stakes for how utilities model future load scenarios. The load growth is not theoretical. It is being felt already in regions with large cloud footprints. In Northern Virginia, one of the world's most concentrated data center markets, the power demand from data centers is projected to exceed grid capacity within a few years. AI is a major factor in that growth. It is not the only cause, but it is the fastest-rising one. This is the signal. Other regions will follow. The response to this challenge cannot be avoidance. AI is not optional infrastructure. It is becoming foundational. Utilities will serve it, or someone else will. What matters is how it is integrated. What matters is how the planning, pricing, and provisioning of energy evolves to meet this new category of demand. There is also a second implication. Utilities are not only suppliers of power. They are also consumers of AI. As grid operations become more automated, as asset management becomes more predictive, as customer service becomes more conversational, utilities will deploy models too. These deployments will not always require frontier training, but they will require inference. They will require real-time evaluation of logs, images, and sensor streams. They will run continuously. They will be embedded in control systems and decision support tools. That means utilities are not only serving the demand. They are participating in it. This dual role introduces complexity. Utilities must now understand the compute behind the models they consume. They must estimate the power cost of their own digital strategies. They must optimize where and how inference happens. Does it run in the cloud? Does it run on edge hardware? Does it require GPU acceleration or can it be distilled into smaller formats? These decisions will shape not only IT budgets, but physical energy use. There is an opportunity here. Utilities have always planned around known systems. They plan for heating and cooling. They plan for lighting and industrial machinery. They plan for electric vehicles. They can also plan for AI --- if they understand its profile. AI training can be scheduled. It can be shaped by pricing signals. It can be constrained by regulation. It can be informed by the carbon intensity of the grid. The same principles that apply to other loads can apply here. What is needed is visibility, coordination, and modeling. Utilities that build that capability will not only manage risk. They will become trusted partners in the AI transition. There is one more point. Not all compute is equal. Not all energy use delivers the same value. AI models that are trained on clean, governed data require less energy to produce useful results. Inference systems that use efficient architectures consume fewer resources. Batch scheduling of workloads can reduce peak loads. Distributed inference can reduce transmission loss. In other words, the structure behind the system affects the energy profile of the system. This is where utilities can lead. By applying the same operational rigor to digital systems that they apply to physical ones, they can drive efficiency. They can optimize not only generation and distribution, but also the digital behaviors that drive consumption. AI is a system, like any other. It has inputs, outputs, efficiencies, and waste. What we choose to measure, we can improve. What we choose to structure, we can scale. The path forward requires understanding this new class of infrastructure, and designing for it the way we design for everything else --- with clarity, with modeling, and with intent.
### The Coming Data Drought Artificial intelligence runs on data. Not just any data, but high-quality, human-generated data.

Every breakthrough in large language models has relied on the availability of text, images, and structured information that people have written, labeled, or curated. These materials form the foundation of today's AI systems. What few realize is that this foundation has limits. Unlike compute, which can be scaled with more chips, or memory, which can be scaled with more hardware, human-generated data cannot be created on demand. It is the product of years, decades, and centuries of recorded experience. It lives in books, websites, code repositories, documentation, logs, transcripts, and historical records. And most of it has already been consumed. Research estimates place the global supply of usable human-generated text data at approximately 300 trillion tokens. This includes everything from Wikipedia articles to forum posts to digitized books. When you remove duplicates, filter for quality, and ensure that it is suitable for training, that number shrinks. Most high-performing models have already consumed large portions of this corpus. At current trends, the usable stock of public training data will be exhausted between 2026 and 2028. This is not speculative. It is based on the actual training behavior of today's frontier models. Some models are overtrained, meaning they are exposed to the same data multiple times in order to maximize inference efficiency. Others are trained on increasingly narrow domains, pushing the limits of what the training set can provide. As these practices expand, the remaining supply of fresh, high-quality data will decline even faster. This creates a new kind of scarcity. The bottleneck for the next generation of AI may not be hardware. It may be information. Compute can be rented, purchased, or manufactured. Human experience cannot. Once the available public data is consumed, models will either begin to plateau or turn inward --- training on outputs of previous models, a process that carries real risks. The loss of originality, the recycling of synthetic content, and the accumulation of errors will lead to a decline in quality and utility. This phenomenon has already been observed in image generation models trained on AI-generated art. The output becomes uniform, derivative, and disconnected from reality. This creates a moment of opportunity for industries like power and utilities. These sectors generate data that is not public. It is operational, proprietary, and grounded in physical systems. It includes maintenance logs, SCADA traces, fault analyses, outage tickets, engineering reports, and customer interactions. These materials are often unstructured or semi-structured. They are not typically accessible through search engines. They are not part of public training sets. This makes them rare and valuable. The uniqueness of utility data lies not in its format, but in its context. Unlike generic text from the internet, utility data is connected to physical processes. A transformer alert corresponds to an actual event. A dispatch record reflects a real decision. These data points are grounded in time, place, and consequence. They are rich with patterns that cannot be found in synthetic benchmarks or open datasets. As the AI ecosystem begins to feel the limits of publicly available training material, it will turn toward domains like this. Utilities will see more requests from external parties --- vendors, researchers, consultants --- asking to use operational data to improve or fine-tune models. These requests will be framed as collaborative. Some will carry real value. Others will carry risk. The challenge is not whether to share data. The challenge is knowing what is being asked. Without clear lineage, it is difficult to separate raw data from sensitive data. Without clear contracts, it is difficult to control how a model trained on utility data behaves once it is deployed elsewhere. Without careful planning, utilities may find themselves in a position where their operational records power tools they cannot audit or influence. The other side of this challenge is internal. Utilities themselves can use this moment to gain an advantage. By treating their data as strategic capital, they can fine-tune models on their own experience. They can build digital tools that understand the specifics of their geography, their assets, and their workflows. They can develop systems that are not just trained on human language, but trained on utility knowledge. Doing so requires structure. It requires metadata that captures definitions and changes over time. It requires systems that can surface the right subset of data for a given task. It requires governance policies that protect customer privacy, operational integrity, and compliance. None of this is new. Utilities have been doing it for decades in the context of grid management. The difference now is that the stakes have shifted. Data is no longer a byproduct. It is the raw material for intelligence. The risk of doing nothing is not just falling behind. It is having others define your future. As generative models become more common in engineering, planning, and operations, the quality of their output will depend on what they were trained on. If those models were trained elsewhere --- on data from different climates, different grids, different policies --- they may offer confident answers that do not fit your reality. This is how fragmentation starts. It begins when decisions are made using generic models trained on other people's data. It accelerates when teams rely on those decisions without verification. It becomes dangerous when automation enters the control loop without understanding the context in which it operates. Utilities can prevent that. They can build models that reflect their own conditions. They can train AI on their own records. They can anchor predictions in the physical realities of their assets. That work cannot happen without preparation. The data must be cleaned. The lineage must be restored. The access must be governed. And the value must be understood. The coming data drought will change the way industries think about information. It will reward those who have unique, high-integrity, well-structured operational records. Utilities are in that position now. They must decide what to do with it.
